Keeping an eye out for Phishing Attempts

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers try to trick people into revealing sensitive information such as passwords or financial details by pretending to be someone they trust, often through fake emails or websites. If you would like to learn more about phishing and how to protect yourself, you can visit the Type of Social Engineeringpage for further details.

Recognizing a Suspicious Email

It's often recommended to look for spelling mistakes or grammatical errors as a sign of phishing. While this might help in some cases, it's not enough on its own. Here are three important clues that can help you identify a phishing attempt:

  • Copycat Behavior

    Many attackers mimic everyday communication styles and company formats to make their message look familiar. If the greeting is vague, like ‚ÄúDear user‚Äù or ‚ÄúValued client,‚Äù it might be a sign that the sender doesn't actually know you

  • Sense of Pressure

    Creating panic or urgency is a classic phishing tactic. If a message urges you to act immediately, such as completing a task before a deadline, take a moment to pause and question it.

  • False Authority

    Messages may appear to come from a known person or institution, such as a manager, a colleague, or a trusted company. This is done to gain your trust quickly and lower your guard.

  • Too Good to Be True

    Attackers sometimes try to trick users with offers that are too good to be true. If an email offers you free gifts, unexpected cash prizes or extraordinary deals, there's a good chance it's a trap. These types of messages are usually aimed at getting you to take action without careful consideration.

Take a Moment to Think

Don't panic if you click on a phishing email. It can happen to anyone. Just keep these key points in mind to stay safe.

  • Control What You Share

    Check your privacy settings regularly and avoid sharing personal details online unless absolutely necessary.

  • Be Aware of Warning Signs

    Watch for urgent language, impersonation of trusted individuals or companies, and messages filled with errors ‚Äî these are common red flags.

  • Understand Internal Guidelines

    Familiarize yourself with your organisation's cybersecurity policies so you know how to react if something goes wrong.

  • Use Trusted Channels

    When you encounter suspicious situations, verify through trusted sources. Seek help from official websites, phone numbers, or people whose reliability you are sure of.
